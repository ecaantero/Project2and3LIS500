Library Resources

Overview

This library resource page provides an introduction to key concepts that help us better understand identity, equity, and bias within technology. It explores how implicit bias, the unconscious attitudes and stereotypes we all carry, can influence our decisions and interactions, often without our awareness. The page also introduces key terms such as intersectionality, which recognizes how overlapping identities such as race, gender, class, and ability shape people’s unique experiences of privilege and discrimination. Additionally, it highlights the Implicit Bias Test (IBT) as a tool for reflecting on automatic associations we may hold.

Key Terms

Implicit Bias Test (IBT)

The Implicit Bias Test (IBT), also known as the Implicit Association Test (IAT), was developed in 1998 by three scientists: Dr. Tony Greenwald, Dr. Mahzarin Banaji, and Dr. Brian Nosek. The Implicit Bias Test is a psychological assessment designed to measure unconscious biases. It shows the automatic associations we make between people and certain traits. The test measures how quickly you associate certain groups (such as- race, gender, age) with positive or negative words or concepts. If you respond faster to one pairing than another, the test interprets that as a possible implicit bias.

Intersectionality

Intersectionality means recognizing that peoples experiences arent shaped by just one part of who they are. Things like race, gender, class, or ability can overlap, creating unique challenges or advantages for each person.

Implicit Bias

Implicit bias is an automatic, unconscious attitude or stereotype that shapes how we think about and treat others. It’s a quick conclusion your brain makes without you even realizing it. These biases often develop from what we absorb through culture, media, family, and personal experiences. Even if we consciously reject certain stereotypes, implicit biases can still influence our decisions and behavior without our awareness.
